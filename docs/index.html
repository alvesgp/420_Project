Non Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease & Fibrosis Transcriptomics

BNFO 420 Capstone

1 Abstract

Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is a condition in which excess fat accumulates in the liver and can progress to more severe stages such as inflammation, fibrosis, and cirrhosis. Fibrosis is characterized by the excessive buildup of extracellular matrix (ECM) components, which disrupts the normal structure and function of liver tissue. As fibrosis worsens, these structural changes can significantly impair liver function. Because of this, understanding the molecular changes that occur during fibrosis progression is important for identifying potential biomarkers and therapeutic targets. In this study, we analyzed RNA-seq data from the GSE135251 dataset, which contains liver biopsy samples from 216 human patients. These samples were classified into three groups: control, early fibrosis, and moderate fibrosis. We hypothesized that as the fibrosis stage increases, the expression of genes involved in extracellular matrix organization would also increase. To test this, raw count data were processed using DESeq2 to identify differentially expressed genes between early and moderate fibrosis samples. Principal component analysis (PCA) was used to examine overall transcriptomic differences across all samples and to see how the groups clustered. The results of the differential expression analysis were visualized using a volcano plot, which highlights significantly upregulated and downregulated genes, and a heatmap showing the expression patterns of the most significant genes. In addition, Gene Ontology (GO) enrichment analysis was performed using clusterProfiler to identify biological processes that were overrepresented among the differentially expressed genes. Our results showed clear transcriptomic differences between control and fibrosis samples, while differences between early and moderate fibrosis were present but less pronounced. GO enrichment analysis revealed that genes associated with extracellular matrix organization were significantly upregulated in moderate fibrosis. These findings support our hypothesis and suggest that fibrosis progression is associated with increased expression of ECM related genes, which may play a key role in disease severity.

2 Introduction

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is a condition where excess fat builds up in the liver. It is one of the most common liver diseases worldwide and affects a large portion of the population (Younossi et al., 2016). NAFLD is closely linked to metabolic conditions such as obesity, insulin resistance, and systemic inflammation, making it an important part of cardiometabolic disease. As the disease progresses, it can lead to more serious conditions, including inflammation, fibrosis, and eventually cirrhosis (Friedman et al., 2018). Because of how common it is and how it connects to other health problems, NAFLD is considered a major public health concern.

NAFLD develops gradually and moves through different stages. It usually starts with simple fat accumulation in the liver, but over time it can progress to inflammation and fibrosis. Among these stages, fibrosis is especially important because it is strongly linked to disease severity and long-term outcomes (Angulo et al., 2015). Fibrosis refers to the buildup of scar tissue in the liver, which can interfere with normal liver function. This makes it important to understand how fibrosis develops and what biological processes are involved.

The development of NAFLD is not caused by a single factor. Instead, it is explained by the “multiple-hit” hypothesis, which suggests that several processes happen at the same time to drive disease progression (Buzzetti et al., 2016). These include fat accumulation, inflammation, oxidative stress, and other cellular changes. Inflammation plays a major role by damaging liver cells and activating pathways that lead to fibrosis (Tilg & Moschen, 2010; Koyama & Brenner, 2017). These inflammatory signals are connected to fibrosis through biological pathways that control how the liver responds to injury and repair (Seki & Schwabe, 2015). Over time, repeated injury and inflammation lead to long-term structural changes in the liver. One of the main features of fibrosis is the buildup of the extracellular matrix (ECM). The ECM is made up of proteins such as collagen that normally help support tissue structure. However, during chronic liver injury, this process becomes unbalanced and leads to too much ECM being produced (Wynn, 2008). Hepatic stellate cells, which are normally inactive, become activated during liver damage and start producing large amounts of ECM (Tsuchida & Friedman, 2017). This process is a key driver of fibrosis and has been widely described as the main mechanism behind scar tissue formation in the liver (Schuppan & Afdhal, 2008). As ECM accumulates, it replaces healthy liver tissue and disrupts normal liver function (Bataller & Brenner, 2005). As fibrosis progresses, ECM continues to accumulate, making the liver stiffer and less able to function properly (Acharya et al., 2021).

In addition to structural changes, metabolic dysfunction also plays an important role in NAFLD. Insulin resistance is one of the key factors, as it causes the liver to store more fat and affects normal metabolic processes (Samuel & Shulman, 2012). Fat in the liver comes from several sources, including dietary intake, fat stored in adipose tissue, and fat produced within the liver itself (Donnelly et al., 2005). Because of these processes, NAFLD is strongly linked to conditions such as obesity and type 2 diabetes (Bugianesi et al., 2005; Tilg et al., 2017). These metabolic changes can make inflammation and fibrosis worse, creating a cycle that drives disease progression.

As NAFLD progresses, changes also occur at the molecular level. Gene expression patterns in the liver shift over time, with a decrease in genes involved in normal metabolism and an increase in genes related to inflammation and tissue remodeling (Browning & Horton, 2004). These changes help explain how the disease develops and progresses at a deeper level. Studying these gene expression patterns can provide important insights into the biological mechanisms behind NAFLD.

Transcriptomic profiling provides a way to study these changes in detail. RNA sequencing (RNA-seq) is a powerful method that allows researchers to measure gene expression across the entire genome (Wang et al., 2009). This makes it possible to identify which genes are more or less active in different disease stages. Standard workflows help ensure that RNA-seq data is analyzed accurately and consistently (Conesa et al., 2016), while modern pipelines make it easier to identify genes that are differentially expressed between groups (Nagai et al., 2024). These tools allow researchers to connect changes in gene expression to biological processes such as extracellular matrix organization.

Publicly available datasets provide valuable opportunities to study NAFLD using real patient samples. The GSE135251 dataset includes liver biopsy samples across different fibrosis stages and has been widely used to study gene expression changes in NAFLD (Govaere et al., 2020). Previous studies using this dataset have shown that fibrosis progression is associated with increased expression of genes involved in extracellular matrix organization and tissue remodeling (Govaere et al., 2020). These findings suggest that ECM plays a key role in fibrosis progression and highlight the importance of studying these pathways.
